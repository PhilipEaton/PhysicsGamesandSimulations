Magnetic Field Around a Straight Wire

Adjust the conventional current in a long straight wire and watch the surrounding magnetic field loops respond in a 3D-style view.

How to use

Use the current slider to change the current in the wire. The blue arrow shows the direction of conventional current. The purple circular arrows show the direction and relative strength of the magnetic field.

The animation makes the field direction easier to see, but you can pause it, step it forward one frame at a time, or use the numeric table below the diagram instead. Keyboard users can tab to the sliders and buttons. On the canvas, use the arrow keys to rotate the view and Shift + arrow keys to make larger rotations.

Interactive field region

A long straight wire is drawn through the center of a three-dimensional coordinate view. A blue arrow on the wire indicates conventional current direction. Purple circular arrows wrap around the wire to indicate the magnetic field direction. The table below gives numerical field values at several radii.

Informational Readout

Current is 8 amperes upward. Magnetic field circles counterclockwise when viewed from above. The closest displayed radius has a field of 160 microtesla.

Magnetic field data table

The table uses the absolute value of current, so direction is handled separately by the right-hand rule. For direction, "from above" means when viewed from the current coming directly at you.

Physics notes

  • The magnetic field is strongest close to the wire and gets weaker as distance from the wire increases.
  • Doubling the current doubles the magnetic field at every distance shown.
  • Use the right-hand rule: point your right thumb along the conventional current, and your fingers curl in the magnetic field direction.
  • At zero current, the field loops fade because an ordinary wire with no current would not create a magnetic field.
